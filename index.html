Position Statement

I approach engineering design like I approach jazz — with the application of practice, collaboration and creativity to express oneself.

"while the bridges I build collapse, I still approach design as applied creativity and an expression of my knowledge."

CIV102 · Structures & Materials · Fall 2025

Matboard Bridge

Design and construction of a load-bearing bridge from matboard — optimized against failure by shear, flexure, and buckling. Tested to failure under a point load at midspan.

annotation ✦

This was vocabulary in action. I couldn't start designing until I reviewed the failure equations — buckling, shear, flexure. The professor's initial design was my Chet Baker transcription: I listened, decoded the logic, then played something new. My I-beam cross section came from there. The collapse told me more than the test itself.

Concepts, Tools, Models & Frameworks

Frame Structural Requirements Analysis
+

What it is: Defining the load capacity requirements using beam theory — establishing the target failure force and the relevant failure modes (shear, flexure, local buckling) that bound the design space.

How I used it: We were given a load requirement and material constraints. I used the provided failure equations to bound the design space before sketching any cross-section — treating each equation as a "chord change" that constrained what was possible.

Assessment: Highly effective as a starting frame. Without it, we would have been improvising without a key. The limitation is that it requires understanding the equations before you can use them as constraints — the vocabulary must come first.

Diverge Morphological Chart
+

What it is: A matrix that breaks a design into functional sub-systems (e.g. top flange shape, web geometry, bottom flange) and generates variants for each — then combines them into novel concepts.

How I used it: Used to generate cross-section shape variants (I-beam, T-beam, box section, trapezoidal web). Allowed us to explore a systematic range without anchoring on any single concept early.

Assessment: Very strong for structured divergence. The risk is that it can produce physically implausible combinations — pairing it with a quick feasibility screen is essential.

Converge Analytical Failure Comparison
+

What it is: Applying the failure equations to each candidate cross-section to predict its failure load. The design with the highest predicted failure load (within mass constraints) converges as the selected concept.

How I used it: Ran failure predictions for our top three cross-section variants. The I-beam scored highest across all three failure modes. Provided a rigorous, quantitative basis for the decision.

Assessment: The clearest convergence tool available in this context — the math does the judging. The key limitation: it only works if the failure model accurately captures the physical behaviour, which it doesn't always (local glue joint failures were not modelled).

Rehabilitative Knee Sleeve

A wearable biomedical device addressing post-surgical knee rehabilitation through targeted compression, joint stabilisation, and ergonomic material selection.

annotation ✦

The hardest part was the framing. A knee is a system, not a part — and users are patients, not operators. The empathy map forced me to slow down and listen before I diverged. This is the "listening in the ensemble" of engineering: let the stakeholder solo before you play.

Concepts, Tools, Models & Frameworks

FrameEmpathy Mapping
+

What it is: A structured tool to capture stakeholder experiences across four dimensions: what they say, think, do, and feel. Used to understand unmet needs beyond what a requirements list captures.

How I used it: Conducted informal interviews with physiotherapy students and generated an empathy map that revealed the primary pain point was not physical support — it was compliance. Patients don't wear uncomfortable devices consistently.

Assessment: Indispensable for biomedical design. Forces you to stop designing for the condition and start designing for the person. The limitation: maps can be vague if interviews aren't specific enough.

DivergeBrainstorming + Biomimicry
+

What it is: Unstructured idea generation followed by biomimicry — looking to biological systems (ligament structure, compression gradients in muscle) for inspiration for textile and mechanical solutions.

How I used it: Explored knee-brace mechanisms from spider silk tensioning to graduated compression socks. Biomimicry pushed us toward a layered textile system that mimics the multi-ligament structure of the knee.

Assessment: Biomimicry was the most generative frame — it broke us out of "conventional device" thinking. The challenge: biological analogies aren't always structurally viable.

RepresentLow-Fidelity Prototype
+

What it is: A physical approximation of the design concept using accessible materials (foam, elastic, velcro) to test form, fit, and initial function before committing to final materials.

How I used it: Built a foam-and-elastic prototype that demonstrated the compression gradient concept. Allowed us to test on a volunteer knee and confirm that the lateral stabilisation panel position was off — critical feedback before committing to fabric.

Assessment: Essential in biomedical design where physical comfort matters. A drawing cannot tell you if something fits. The limitation: foam doesn't replicate real textile behaviour — there's a gap between the prototype and the intended material.

Loose Outlets — Praxis I
ESC102 · Praxis I · Fall 2025

The Loose Outlet Problem

A quiet, invisible hazard: millions of North American outlets gradually loosen from their boxes, creating fire risk and shock hazard. We designed a retention mechanism to prevent this.

annotation ✦

The chord changes moment: we initially wrote our requirement as "a percentage improvement in retention." That's a vague chord — too open, like playing with no changes. I suggested switching to a specific Newton force target. Everything sharpened after that. The design space narrowed, and ideas became comparable.

Concepts, Tools, Models & Frameworks

FrameHow Might We (HMW) Framing
+

What it is: A reframing technique that converts problem statements into open-ended design questions using the structure "How Might We…" — to open solution space without anchoring to a specific answer.

How I used it: Our original problem statement was too solution-specific ("design a clip"). HMW forced us to step back: How might we prevent outlet migration from the junction box? This opened material, geometric, and fastening approaches that a clip-focused framing would have excluded.

Assessment: A light, high-leverage tool. Best applied early — it shapes every downstream decision. The risk is framing too broadly ("HMW make electricity safe?") or too narrowly ("HMW hold this one screw?").

ConvergeMeasurement Matrix (Pugh Chart)
+

What it is: A concept selection matrix that scores design concepts against weighted requirements criteria. Allows structured, auditable comparison of multiple concepts.

How I used it: After defining our retention force requirement (a specific Newton value), we scored three concepts: a tension-tab insert, a friction-wedge plate, and a threaded collar. The matrix revealed that the friction-wedge scored highest on ease of installation — our primary user constraint.

Assessment: The quality of this tool depends entirely on the quality of the requirements. With vague requirements (our original % improvement), the matrix was useless. With the Newton-force requirement, it became meaningful.

RepresentDesign One-Pager
+

What it is: A double-sided letter-sized design summary that presents the opportunity, the design, and the evidence in a concise, visually organized format for an external audience.

How I used it: Prepared for the Praxis I Showcase. The one-pager forced us to reduce the project to its essential logic: the problem (outlets loosen), the mechanism (friction wedge), and the evidence (retention force measured in Newtons).

Assessment: Extremely useful for communication clarity. The constraint of one page forces prioritization — you learn what matters when you're forced to cut. I would use this earlier in the process as a self-check on clarity of thought.

Variable Swim Resistance — Praxis II
ESC102 · Praxis II · Winter 2026

Variable Swim Resistance Device

Design of an adjustable hydrodynamic drag device for competitive swimmer training — allowing athletes to precisely control resistance as a training variable, not just a fixed constraint.

annotation ✦

This project taught me that the design space can be designed. The design space IS the variable resistance — we weren't just designing a device, we were designing the space within which a swimmer could train. That's a chord change: choosing the key signature, not just playing in it.

Concepts, Tools, Models & Frameworks

FrameOpportunity Framing + Stakeholder Map
+

What it is: A structured tool for identifying who is affected by the design opportunity and how — mapping primary users, secondary users, experts, and affected parties before defining requirements.

How I used it: Mapped competitive swimmers, coaches, and equipment managers. Key insight: coaches don't swim — they design training programs. The real user of the resistance device is the coach's protocol, not just the athlete's body. This reframed the adjustability requirement.

Assessment: Critical for avoiding the "design for the obvious user" trap. The expanded map revealed a requirement (coach-configurable levels) that no athlete-only interview would have surfaced.

DivergeSCAMPER + Concept Sketching
+

What it is: SCAMPER is a structured creativity technique: Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse. Applied to existing swim resistance equipment to generate novel variants.

How I used it: Applied SCAMPER to existing drag suits and parachute trainers. "Modify" led to the concept of a variable-aperture fin array; "Combine" combined a parachute's deployment mechanism with a rigid-body frame. Produced 12 sketched concepts in a single session.

Assessment: More generative than free brainstorming — the prompts break satisficing behaviour ("the first idea that works"). The weakness: some SCAMPER prompts feel forced when applied rigidly.

ConvergeWeighted Decision Matrix
+

What it is: A concept selection tool where criteria are weighted by importance before scoring, allowing nuanced comparison when requirements have different priorities.

How I used it: Four criteria: adjustable drag range (weight 0.35), durability in chlorine water (0.25), ease of don/doff (0.25), cost (0.15). Our fin-array concept scored highest overall despite not scoring highest on any single criterion — the weighting revealed its balanced strength.

Assessment: Superior to an unweighted Pugh chart when criteria genuinely differ in importance. The subjectivity of the weights is a limitation — we debated the drag range weight extensively, which itself was a valuable design conversation.

RepresentFunctional Prototype + Showcase One-Pager
+

What it is: A working physical prototype that demonstrates the core functional behaviour (in this case, adjustable drag generation in water), combined with a double-sided showcase summary.

How I used it: Built and pool-tested a 3D-printed fin array with an adjustable pitch mechanism. Measured drag at three resistance settings using a force gauge. The one-pager presented the mechanism, the measurements, and the training protocol implications.

Assessment: The pool test was the most important moment of the project — theoretical drag calculations did not predict the actual force profile. Testing revealed a non-linear response at high pitch angles that changed our recommended training protocol.
